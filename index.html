Course Description

Generative AI is changing software development. This course teaches students to build and manage applications using AI agents, a process known as "vibe coding." Students will learn the foundations of Large Language Models (LLMs) and develop the skills to prototype and deploy AI products.

Course Objectives

  • 1. Explain Foundations: Define LLM architectures, tokens, and context windows.
  • 2. Apply Workflows: Use Google, OpenAI, and Anthropic tools to generate functional code.
  • 3. Analyze Strategy: Evaluate the economics and legal implications of AI applications.
  • 4. Deploy Products: Take a project from a concept to a live development state.

Phase 1: Foundations

Week 1: AI History and Market Overview

  • Topics: The 3,000-year history of AI, from the foundations of logic and Al-Khwarizmi's algebra to Alan Turing’s 1950 "Imitation Game." The transition into modern Large Language Models and a current market overview of the "Big Three" ecosystems—Google, OpenAI, and Anthropic. Learning Objective Connection: Builds foundational literacy and establishes the market context for selecting development tools.
  • Activity: Account setup and basic prompting to compare model responses across the three main platforms.
  • Helpful Material: Alan Turing: "Computing Machinery and Intelligence". Watch the AlphaGo documentary.

Week 2: AI Literacy and Quality Detection

  • Topics: Strategies for identifying model hallucinations and "AI Slop." Analysis of current workforce adoption trends and the broader societal effects of automated cognitive labor. Students will learn to distinguish between thoughtfully orchestrated AI output and low value synthetic content. Learning Objective Connection: Directly supports the objective of evaluating AI outputs for business readiness and reliability.
  • Activity: Reviewing AI outputs to identify functional errors and aesthetic indicators of poor quality.
  • Helpful Material: Andrej Karpathy: "Intro to Large Language Models".

Week 3: Compute and AI Economics

  • Topics: The physical backbone of AI, including GPUs and data center logistics. An analysis of token based pricing models and how underlying hardware availability dictates the cost and speed of AI applications. Learning Objective Connection: Connects technical token logic to the business objective of financial feasibility.
  • Activity: Calculating the cost of a hypothetical AI product based on different token usage patterns.
  • Helpful Material: "The Bitter Lesson" by Rich Sutton.

Week 4: How AI Thinks: Context and Accuracy

  • Topics: Understanding the "next token" prediction model. Controlling model behavior and output randomness. How to tune a model’s tone and accuracy for specific business use cases. Learning Objective Connection: Provides the base knowledge required to understand why AI produces specific results and how to steer it towards business-quality answers.
  • Activity: Group discussion on managing model "context" to prevent logical errors in long documents.
  • Helpful Material: "Attention Is All You Need".

Week 5: Configuring AI Workflows

  • Topics: Advanced prompt strategies including "Chain of Thought" and practical examples of steering model logic. Understanding "system instructions" and how to build a reliable context for an AI employee. Learning Objective Connection: Teaches the core skill of building reliable business logic without deep coding.
  • Activity: Designing a system prompt and instruction set for a specialized AI advisor.
  • Helpful Material: "Chain of Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in LLMs".

Week 6: APIs and AI Agents

  • Topics: Transitioning from chat interfaces to API driven development. Introduction to function calling, tool use, and the logic required to build autonomous agents that can interact with external web data. Learning Objective Connection: Prepares students to connect their project to real world data sources.
  • Activity: Mapping the required API connections for an autonomous event planning agent.
  • Helpful Material: "ReAct: Synergizing Reasoning and Acting in LLMs".

Week 7: Midterm Exam

Phase 2: Applied Development

Week 8: Project Ideation and Planning

  • Topics: From concept to technical architecture. Students will define the scope of their final solo project and select the ecosystem (Google, OpenAI, or Anthropic) that best fits their product requirements. Learning Objective Connection: Begins the final objective of launching a solo product.
  • Project Step: Finalize project proposal and ecosystem selection.
  • Helpful Material: "What is Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)?".

Week 9: Frontend and Asset Generation

  • Topics: Using AI to bridge the gap between wireframes and functional code. Utilizing multi-modal models to generate branding, icons, and professional media assets for the final application. Learning Objective Connection: Focuses on the objective of generating functional, professional grade user experiences.
  • Project Step: Generate the initial UI interface for the solo application.
  • Helpful Material: "Generative UI and the Future of UX".

Week 10: Application Logic

  • Topics: Practical AI assisted development. Using agents to build, debug, and iterate on application code while utilizing Git as a primary safety net during building. Learning Objective Connection: Central to the goal of mastering AI assisted development workflows.
  • Project Step: Build core logic and connect prompts to data sources.
  • Helpful Material: "The Twelve-Factor App".

Week 11: Legal and Responsibility

  • Topics: Copyright and Intellectual Property issues surrounding AI outputs. A review of data privacy concerns and the ethics of deploying models into public environments. Learning Objective Connection: Addresses the legal and ethical analysis objective.
  • Project Step: Testing the application for failure modes and bias.
  • Helpful Material: "On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots".

Week 12: Product Management

  • Topics: The operational challenges of AI, including prompt governance, model version drift, and handling the non deterministic nature of AI products in a production environment. Learning Objective Connection: Covers the strategic objective of long term product management in the AI era.
  • Project Step: Public deployment of the solo project.
  • Helpful Material: Paul Graham: "How People Get Rich Now".

Week 13: Industry Review

  • Topics: A review of emerging industry trends and a "final polish" workshop for the solo projects before launch.

Week 14: Project Launch

  • Activity: Final project presentations and live demonstrations.
  • Helpful Material: Mustafa Suleyman: "The Coming Wave".

Reference Library

  • Russell and Norvig: "Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach" — The industry-standard textbook for the entire field. Included for students seeking a rigorous, comprehensive foundation in AI history and theory.
  • Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville: "Deep Learning" — The definitive technical guide to neural networks. Included for those seeking a deep dive into the mathematical architecture of modern models.
  • Brian Christian: "The Alignment Problem" — A high-level exploration of the challenge of ensuring AI values match human goals. Included for critical context on the ethical stakes of deployment.
  • Hugging Face: "Official Engineering Blog" — The leading community hub for open-source AI. Included to keep students up-to-date with the latest model releases and engineering best practices.
